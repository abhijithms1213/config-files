###### Normalize being wrong

Tell her: “When you explain, you’re not expected to be 100% correct. Explaining itself is part of learning.”

Even experts revise and correct themselves.

Just like writing a draft before editing, her explanations don’t need to be perfect on the first try. She can refine later.

Celebrate every attempt she makes, even if incomplete. The more she explains, the smoother it will get.

Instead of “What if they judge me?”, think “Every attempt makes me better, even if I mess up.”

*People usually respect someone who tries, even if they stumble.*

# Golden Rule: Effort > Accuracy in the Beginning

## Steps

1. **Appreciate that she tried to explain.**  
2. **Pick one small point she did well and highlight it.**  
3. **Ask a gentle follow-up or give a simple correction.**  

---

## ✅ Example 1 – Technical Concept (Stack)

She says:  
“Stack… I think it’s like… umm, a list where you… you put things in, and you also… maybe remove them… but I’m not sure the order.”

- *Encourage → “Nice! You already got the main idea — it’s a list where you add and remove.”*  
- *Highlight correct → “Yes, it’s definitely about order.”*  
- *Gentle refine → “The special rule is: last in, first out. Like a stack of plates — you take the top one first.”*  

**👉 She hears: “I wasn’t totally wrong. I had the right idea. I just needed one correction.” → Confidence goes up.**  

---

## ✅ Example 2 – Scattered Explanation (Queue)

She says:  
“Queue is… umm… I think like… people wait, but sometimes it’s also like… maybe you can skip? Or… it’s confusing.”

- *Encourage → “Good start — you remembered people waiting, that’s the right image.”*  
- *Simplify → “Think of a movie ticket line — first person in line gets the ticket first.”*  
- *Re-engage → “Can you give me another real-life example of a queue?”*  

**👉 She ends with confidence, not “I don’t know.”**  

---

## ✅ Example 3 – Completely Wrong but Tried (Recursion)

She says:  
“Recursion… I think it’s when you do loops in a different way?”

- *Encourage → “I like that you connected it to loops — recursion does feel like repeating.”*  
- *Redirect gently → “But the special thing is: instead of using a loop, the function calls itself.”*  
- *Anchor with analogy → “Like when you put a mirror in front of another mirror — the reflection repeats itself.”*  

---

## 🧠 Why this works
- **She feels safe to try, even if wrong.**  
- **You reward the attempt, not just the accuracy.**  
- **Gentle correction + analogy helps her remember better.**  

---

- Highlight what’s right.  
- Add one clear correction.  
- Re-engage her with a small question.
